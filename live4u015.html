As smartphones grew increasingly prevalent, QR codes quickly became part of everyday life. People began using them to check bus schedules, get coupons, register for medical visits, and more. During the COVID-19 pandemic, QR codes became especially important for contact tracing, health forms, and digital menus. They became both a key tool and major symbol of the new contactless lifestyle that many people now rely on.

The impact of QR codes goes beyond just saving time. They have changed how people find and use information. Users can access websites, join events, or receive instant updates by simply scanning a code instead of typing long links or filling out forms. In this way, QR codes act as a bridge between the physical and digital worlds while making online interactions faster and easier.

Meanwhile, experts are currently exploring ways to combine QR codes with newer technologies such as artificial intelligence and augmented reality, which could enable even smarter interactive experiences.

From their modest start in car factories to their major role in daily smartphone use, QR codes have demonstrated how a seemingly simple invention can spark significant change. They may be small, but these tiny squares have already dramatically altered the way people all over share and receive information.

Wise Owls and Scheming Snakes, Animals in Myth and Tradition

When you think of a snake, what comes to mind? Many people associate snakes with lies and trickery. This comes from the biblical story of Adam and Eve. In the story, a snake convinces Eve to eat the forbidden fruit, which causes God to kick humans out of paradise. Even today, snake is used to describe people who lie or deceive. And around the world, myths and traditions have inspired a variety of similar beliefs about animals.

Lions Lions are traditionally representations of strength and courage. In ancient Egypt, the warrior goddess Sekhmet was depicted with a lion's head, while in ancient Greece, the Nemean lion was described as one of Hercules' greatest challenges. The association of lions with strength has led to many leaders taking them on as their own symbols. For centuries, lions represented the monarchy in Iran, and the royal emblems of both England and Scotland also feature the big cats.

Eagles Eagles have long been associated with power. In ancient Rome, soldiers carried images of the birds into battle, as they were a symbol of Jupiter, the Romans' chief deity. Similarly, the Greeks saw eagles as having a bond with Zeus, their most powerful god, and in North America, Indigenous tribes believed eagles had a special connection with the Great Spirit.
