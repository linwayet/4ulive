Designed to run, born to feel. Racehorses amaze us with their strength and speed, but physical power is not the only thing that makes them fascinating. They also have extraordinary ways of sensing the world around them. One behavior that many people mistake for a smile reveals just how complex their senses truly are. When horses lift their upper lips and show their teeth, they may appear to be laughing. In reality, this expression has nothing to do with joy. It's a natural reaction known as the Flaman response. By curling their lips, horses draw scents into their nostrils and pass them to a special organ located in the roof of the mouth called the vomeronasal organ or Jacobson's organ. This organ helps horses detect chemical signals that a regular sense of smell cannot. Through these signals, they can recognize other horses, sense emotional states, or gather information about their surroundings. Thus, when horses smile, they are not being friendly or trying to amuse people. They are actually analyzing scent, using a highly specialized sensory system that humans lack. What may look like a funny face is, in fact, one of the most sophisticated ways horses understand the world.
Designed to run, born to feel. Horses have shared a close bond with humans for centuries. Once mainly used for farming and transportation, they are now admired for their beauty, strength, and athletic ability. Among them, racehorses stand out as especially impressive. When they burst forth from the gate, every part of their body moves with one clear purpose, run as fast as possible. Racehorses can run 100 meters in about half the time it takes the fastest humans. This remarkable ability places them among the quickest land animals on Earth. Watching racehorses charge down the track is thrilling because it shows how power, stamina, and instinct work in perfect harmony. Horse's bodies are naturally designed for speed. The lower parts of their legs have almost no muscles. Instead, they are supported by tendons and ligaments that act like springs, which store and release energy with each step. Their long legs increase stride length, while large lungs enable greater oxygen intake, and a powerful heart pumps oxygen-rich blood efficiently. Even their nostrils are specially shaped to maximize airflow during a race. Every feature works together to help them move faster and farther. Diet and training are equally important to a racehorse's performance. Trainers prepare balanced meals with protein, fiber, starch, and fat to provide both immediate and lasting energy. Meanwhile, regular exercise, adequate rest, and proper medical care help keep them in peak condition. Legendary names such as Secretariat, Seabiscuit and Winning Brew remind us that true speed comes from both natural talent and expert care.
