Adopting Fruit Trees, Sharing Harvests and Hopes

Have you ever dreamed of owning a fruit tree while harvesting fruits without needing to grow the tree yourself? Maybe you would enjoy adopting one. Fruit tree adoption allows people to help support a tree grown by farmers by paying a fee. The tree stays in the orchard, while adopters share the harvest and receive information about the tree's growth and care. Farmers manage daily tasks, taking care of the crops by watering, pruning, and protecting them throughout the seasons. In return, adopters receive fresh, organic, and seasonal produce once the harvest time arrives. They can also join special farm activities to experience the joy of farming.

The meaning behind fruit tree adoption is deeper than simply receiving fruit. It creates a bridge that connects city people and rural farmers. Adopters feel connected to agriculture and nature, while farmers gain long-term partners. In addition, adopting fruit trees protects farmers from unstable market prices caused by heavy rain or typhoons. This gives them more freedom to plan ahead and continue sustainable farming practices.

Adopting Fruit Trees: Sharing Harvests and Hopes

Adopting a fruit tree is simple and rewarding. First, choose a farm through an online platform or an official program. Next, select the type of fruit tree you prefer. After paying the adoption fee, your adoption is confirmed. You'll receive regular updates with photos or reports before finally enjoying your share of the fruit.

The Maybagal tribe in Miaoli offers a powerful example. A typhoon once destroyed their homes and damaged farmlands, which left local families with almost nothing. To recover, local people created the adoption program. The funds they raise are used for farming and rebuilding houses.

People joining the My Bagal program can adopt plum trees. Once the fruits are ready for harvest, adopters are invited to the tribe. There they can pick the plums by hand and enjoy tribal culture. Adopters can take part of the harvest home. The remaining plums are collected by farmers and sent to the adopters or turned into delicious jams.

The project hopes to expand in the future. By sharing its warmth with more people, the Maybagal tribe can also continue to sustain its land.
