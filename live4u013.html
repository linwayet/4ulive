The magic of lithium, the white gold that powers the world. Light, powerful, and surprisingly versatile, lithium impacts your life in ways that feel almost magical. You rely on it every time you use your smartphone, laptop, or digital camera because these devices run on lithium batteries. You'll find that lithium gets you where you want to go if you ride in an electric vehicle, which, again, is more than likely powered by a lithium battery. In addition to electronics and cars, this soft white metal powers medical implants and even support satellites orbiting high above the Earth. On a larger scale, lithium batteries keep power stations running securely and steadily by soaking up daytime solar power and then releasing it at night when it is needed.

Nicknamed white gold for its economic worth and silver color, lithium is a paradox, the lightest of metals, soft enough to cut, yet strong enough to shoulder the load of modern technology. The reason why this excellent conductor of heat and electricity floats is that it is less dense than water. When magnesium and aluminum are combined with lithium, an alloy of remarkable strength and lightness is formed, making it ideal in the production of parts for aircraft and high-speed trains. And to protect those parts and keep them running smoothly, durable greases made from lithium fit the bill.

Lithium's uses go far beyond electronics and manufacturing. In psychiatry, it is highly effective for long-term management of bipolar disorder. Research shows that lithium affects the central nervous system, releasing chemicals such as serotonin, the feel-good chemical, which helps regulate moods and emotions. In addition, several studies have documented findings showing that lithium in drinking water lowers rates of suicide and even crime rates. A study in Japan linked lithium in water supplies with lower depression rates and less violence among adolescents.

Lithium also holds good news for the world's aging population. Recent research suggests that low doses of the white gold can help the brain age well with lower incidences of dementia and cognitive decline. This element, found in small amounts in the diets of both animals and humans, may generally improve mental and physical health for everyone.

Depending on the environment, lithium occurs naturally in the groundwater, soil, and certain food. Drinking water in areas with high lithium levels provides an excellent source of this element. Greens like spinach, along with beans, potatoes, and root vegetables, as well as some nuts and seeds, are found to contain some levels of this extraordinary element.
