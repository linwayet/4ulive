Women Who Shaped Science Throughout history, women have played a significant role in shaping scientific advancement. International Women's Day offers the perfect opportunity to celebrate and honor the brilliant women whose research and ideas transformed the world of science. Here are some of their stories. Mary Anning, 1799-1847 As a girl, Mary Anning learned how to collect fossils from her father, an activity that was not generally encouraged in girls in the early 19th century. She went on to find countless fossils, but her most famous discoveries are the first ichthyosaur and first complete skeleton of a plesiosaurus. Her findings expanded scientific understanding of prehistoric life and helped shape early ideas about extinction and evolution. Although her work influenced artists and scientists alike, Anning's contributions were only fully recognized after her death.

Rachel Carson, 1907-1964 The biologist Rachel Carson combined her love of writing with her love for the ocean and the natural world. Her most influential work was her book Silent Spring, 1962, which discussed the dangers of pesticides. The book resulted in an environmental movement and major policy changes, including the creation of the U.S. Environmental Protection Agency. Carson spent her life fighting for stronger environmental protections for the land and seas that she loved so deeply.

Wu Jianxiong, 1912-1997 Chinese-American scientist Wu Jianxiong is known for her work in nuclear physics. After studying in China, she moved to the U.S. To continue her research. In 1944, she joined the Manhattan Project, a collection of scientists who developed the first atomic weapons. She also led a famous experiment that completely changed scientists' understanding of physics. She strongly advocated for women and girls to follow their dreams of studying science until her death in 1997.

Jennifer Doudna, 1964 to present Jennifer Doudna's work on CRISPR changed the field of genetics forever. With her colleague, Emmanuelle Charpentier, she developed a method to edit the DNA of humans, plants, and animals. Her method has the potential to cure genetic diseases before birth, and to fight many illnesses and cancers later in life. It even allows farmers to grow healthier crops. Since this technology raised some ethical concerns, Doudna has also worked to make sure that this powerful tool is used responsibly. She was awarded the Nobel Prize in Chemistry in 2020.

These incredible women are among many who continue to encourage young people to become scientists. As we celebrate their achievements, we also look forward to the discoveries yet to come from the women shaping the science of tomorrow.
