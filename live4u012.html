Air pollution, an invisible threat. From cooking fires to fossil fuels, air pollution is all around us. A mother leans over a wood-burning stove as her baby plays nearby in a cloud of smoke. A family of three on a scooter rides down a busy city street. A group of students walks past a chemical manufacturing plant on their way to school. These everyday scenes hint at a dark truth. Nearly everyone on Earth, 99% of the global population, breathes air that is more polluted than the World Health Organization, WHO, guidelines allow. Air pollution causes around 8 million deaths each year, making it one of the leading risks to human health around the world.

And people from low- and middle-income countries, LMICs, face the highest exposure, suffering almost eight times the mortality rates of high-income countries.

Vehicles, industrial production, and burning fossil fuels cause outdoor air pollution.

Tiny particles are released into the air by these two main types of air pollution. Once breathed into the lungs, these particles enter the bloodstream and can damage the heart, lungs, and other organs, leading to strokes, lung cancer, heart disease, and chronic respiratory illnesses.

Air pollution is responsible for about 1 in 10 deaths worldwide. Tragically, children are especially vulnerable. According to the 2024 State of Global Air Report, 709,000 children died of causes related to air pollution in 2021, representing 15% of all global deaths of children under 5.

Hope remains, however, for change. Global awareness and stricter regulations have already cut deaths from household pollution since 1990, and clean energy is being utilized more frequently for electricity, heating, and transportation. Furthermore, organizations like Washington, D.C.-based Clean Cooking Alliance raise money and partner with other organizations to develop, market, and distribute clean-burning cookstoves in developing countries. Not only are these cook stoves better for people's health, but by reducing cooking time, they can provide economic benefits as well.

Individuals can take simple steps to improve the air quality around them, such as planting plants that purify the air, using an air purifier at home, and switching out chemical cleaning products for natural ones.
