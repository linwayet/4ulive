Threads of Tradition, the story of Taiwan's gaji bags. Gaji bags are colorful net bags with a long and interesting history that reflects Taiwan's cultural roots. They were said to be created during Japanese rule, 1895 to 1945. Farmers in Tainan's Houbi area originally wove them by hand with a plant called Cyperus malaxensis. It is a kind of grass often found in wetlands. These early gaji bags were strong, light, and easy to carry, perfect for holding produce to markets or homes. Because families in the countryside relied on them daily, the bags became a common sight in Taiwanese villages. From the 1960s to the 1970s, new nylon materials took the place of the plant fibers. This change made the bags cheaper and easier to produce.

And factories began mass-producing nylon gauji bags. Soon the bags spread across the island, moving from village farms to urban shopping places. The classic red, green, and blue stripes became their special look, making them easy to spot at markets everywhere. For many years, nearly every household owned at least one of these lasting', event)">long-lasting, low-cost bags. Though considered plain and simple, gauji bags serve as reflections of the hard-working and practical spirit of early Taiwanese society.

Threads of Tradition The Story of Taiwan's Gaji Bags In the 1980s, though, the popularity of gaji bags fell, as plastic shopping bags became cheap and common in many homes. Some people began to think gaji bags looked old-fashioned, believing they were only for older shoppers at traditional markets. However, growing interest in eco-friendly living and local traditions later brought these striped classics back into the spotlight. Designers introduced improved materials, new colors, and stronger handles to fit the needs of modern city life. Several new styles included zippers or small pockets, making the bags both practical and pretty for daily use. Today, these reinvented bags attract buyers for picnics, beach trips, or school use. They are also considered souvenirs for those who like their traditional beauty. The bag's tri-color stripes each have special meanings, red for human kindness, green for farmland, and blue for countryside skies. They reflect Taiwan's farming past and community spirit. Through vivid threads and lasting traditions, gaji bags link each generation and share their long history with the world.
