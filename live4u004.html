St. Patrick's Day, the Irish holiday that paints the world green. On March 17th each year, millions of people celebrate St. Patrick's Day. But how did a holiday marking the death of Ireland's most famous saint transform into the celebration of all things Irish that we know today?

St. Patrick, the patron saint of Ireland, was a real person, but he wasn't actually Irish. He was born in 5th century Britain and was taken to Ireland after being captured and made a slave by Irish pirates. After he escaped, he journeyed back to Britain and became a priest before returning to Ireland to teach Irish pagans about Christianity.

There are numerous legends about this period of his life. The most famous story is that he drove all the snakes out of Ireland. However, modern scientists believe the island was likely never home to snakes in the first place.

In Dublin, which hosts the country's largest St. Patrick's Day celebration, over 500,000 people attend a street parade featuring marching bands, dancers, and community groups. Other Irish towns and cities also hold big celebrations, with live music and Irish dancing among the most popular events.

Shamrocks and the color green are a big part of St. Patrick's Day. Ireland's national flower can be seen decorating everything from cookies to costumes. It has become associated with St. Patrick because stories say he used its three leaves to explain Christianity's Holy Trinity. You'll also see people dressed from head to toe in green.

Today, St. Patrick's Day is a much-loved international event, and some of the biggest celebrations take place outside Ireland. Parades are held in New York, Montreal, Tokyo, and even Buenos Aires. Popular buildings around the world, such as the Sydney Opera House and the Leaning Tower of Pisa, are lit up in green for the day.

In the U.S., which is home to one of the largest Irish populations, a special dye is used to turn the Chicago River bright green every year. Irish Americans celebrate by gathering with family and enjoying traditional dishes like Irish soda bread and lamb stew.

Now it's time to start digging out your greenest clothes. Whether you're Irish or not, St. Patrick's Day offers everyone a chance to celebrate and have fun.
